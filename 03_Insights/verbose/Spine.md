# The spine

*The one argument that runs through all five Parts, so the doc reads as a case, not five disconnected answers. This is the opening of the Solution doc and the thesis on the site's landing page.*

## The thesis (landing / opening)

Leadership has a clean story: completed rides slipped through May, ticked back up the moment congestion pricing shipped, so the pricing is working, so scale it. The story is clean, and it is mostly wrong. Completed rides are not a dial you turn; a finished ride is the last survivor of a long chain, and the charge reaches only a narrow link of it. When I reproduce the headline I get it to the decimal, and then it falls apart in three independent ways. The charge does genuine work in exactly one place, Auto Priority at peak, and almost nothing on the mass tier that carries the volume. Worse, scaling it spends the one structural advantage NY actually has. Under zero-commission the rider's fare *is* the driver's pay, so the charge is the only dial NY owns on driver earnings and it hits rider price-sensitivity the instant it moves; you cannot outbid a subsidy when you take nothing from the fare. The real leak is elsewhere, in the driver who cancels over an unpaid pickup and the rider who gets no quote and silently leaves, and neither is a price the meter can fix. So this is not a "ship it" memo. It is the case for keeping the charge narrow, attacking the real leak, and growing completion with the asset NY does own outright: data and prediction.

## How each Part carries the argument

- **Part 1 frames the chain.** Completed rides decompose into four stages, and a congestion charge is not a general lever; it is a two-signed force living in two specific spots. This is the spine every later Part is hung on, and it predicts the win will be narrow before any data is opened.
- **Part 2 tests the belief and splits the verdict.** Run on the cube, the charge is a real but bounded win on Auto Priority at peak and a thermometer, not a medicine, on the mass tier. The +14.9% is mostly a shrinking denominator, demand that recovered early, and an off-peak version swap. The structural reason it stays small is zero-commission itself.
- **Part 3 names the humans the number hides.** The driver cancelling the ₹67 trip is optimising his hour against an unpaid deadhead the app never showed him; the rider getting no quote is a silent withdrawal from the network loop that is the only thing a marketplace compounds. The leak is behavioural and structural, not a pricing gap.
- **Part 4 answers the constraint.** If price is the wrong lever, what grows completion win-win with no subsidy? A MECE map of the marketplace funnels to one defended lever: supply-shaping, positioning cars ahead of predictable demand using data NY already holds, with committed rides as the staged endgame once density is real.
- **Part 5 stress-tests the analysis that started it all.** Four critiques, each tied to the ship decision, showing how a correct calculation produced a wrong recommendation, and what signal this dataset structurally cannot contain, ending on the one thing I would insist on before any city-wide ship: a real randomized holdout.

## The close

Then the ≤5-line summary for ops and exec: keep the charge narrow, attack the real leak, build toward supply-shaping, hold the line on a blanket rollout, and watch completed rides per day counted not ratioed, tier by tier.
