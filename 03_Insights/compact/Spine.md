# The spine

## The thesis (landing / opening)

Leadership has a clean story: completed rides slipped through May, ticked back up the moment congestion pricing shipped, so the pricing is working, so scale it. The story is clean, but it is mostly wrong. Completed rides are not a dial you turn; a finished ride is the last survivor of a long chain, and the charge reaches only a narrow link of it. When I reproduce the headline I get it to the decimal, and then it falls apart in three independent ways. The charge does genuine work in exactly one place- for Auto Priority at peak hours, and almost nothing on the mass tier that carries the volume. Scaling it would spend the one structural advantage NY actually has. Under zero-commission the rider's fare *is* the driver's pay, so the charge is the only dial NY owns on driver earnings and it hits rider price-sensitivity the instant it moves. You cannot outbid a subsidy when you take nothing from the fare. The real leak is elsewhere, in the driver who cancels over an unpaid pickup and the rider who gets no quote and silently leaves, and neither is a price the meter can fix. So this is not a "ship it" memo. It is the case for keeping the charge narrow, attacking the real leak, and growing completion with the asset NY does own outright: data and prediction.
